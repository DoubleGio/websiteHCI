Story

Mission

ADAM & AOKI is on a mission to make clothes smarter.
For thousands of years we’ve focused on practically in clothing and that time has come to an end.
It’s time to start wearing clothes that not only look and feel great, but also help us to overcome everyday challenges like sunburn.

Improving your everyday life

Vision

We believe that as a business, our aim should be to try to make the world a better place.
That’s why our vision is to produce sustainable smart clothing on a global scale that is specially designed to help solve millions of real-world problems, each and every day.
#changeit. If you’re unhappy or upset with something, be the agent of change.
Transparency. No good ever came of lies and secrecy. Just ask Romeo & Juliet.
Doing good. Solving real world problems is what gets us out of bed in the morning and we’d like to keep it thatway.
Persevere. Let’s agree to never give up on our individual and shared challenges.
Sustainability. We ❤ planet earth, but for it to keep being planet earth we need to take care of it.

How the company came to be

Founder's story continued

Fortunately I was - and luckily still am - surrounded by some amazing people.
My Grandparents, who lived through WWII, were always on hand valuable and candid advice.
My brother Ben, who has more often that not been a voice of reason.
And there was Gerry, one of my mother’s best friend and an unofficial mentor to me.

A life-changing experience

Gerry and his influence

I admired Gerry from the get go.
A gentlemen, championing the fight against important causes like elder abuse and gay rights, and an unintended comedy genius.
He was once at a ceremony with the then president Bill Clinton, and beamed as he told how he was close enough to take great photos.
He couldn’t wait to get them developed. Only problem was he’d forgotten to put film in his camera.

Skin cancer fight

Risk factors of UV rays

One of the first times I met him he parked his car in central London a spot that probably wasn’t meant to be parked in.
Having stepped out the car, he walked over to the ticket machine.
A few buttons later, the machine broke and spat out a bunch of pound coins but no ticket.
He proclaimed that he thought “it would probably be ok” and proceeded to give me the change.
The act typified his unselfishness.
Sadly Gerry died of cancer in 2013.
As a young man he had skin cancer (melanoma), developed from too much skin damage acquired from excessive sunbathing.
Even though he beat skin cancer a young man, he died of lung cancer — which was caused by melanoma cancer cells that had remained from his first battle with cancer.
